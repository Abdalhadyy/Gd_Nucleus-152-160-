Section 02
Understanding the Physics
⚛️

Interacting Boson Model (IBM-1)

IBM-1 describes collective nuclear excitations by treating correlated nucleon pairs as bosons. The model uses two types: s-bosons (spin-0, spherical) and d-bosons (spin-2, deformed). The total boson number N = Nₙ/2 + Nₚ/2 determines the model space.

Despite treating nucleons as pairs rather than individually, IBM-1 successfully reproduces energy spectra and electromagnetic transition rates across a wide range of nuclei.

🔄

Shape Phase Transition

As neutron number increases, nuclei undergo a structural evolution from spherical to axially deformed shapes. This is not a gradual change — it resembles a quantum phase transition, occurring sharply near a critical neutron number.

In Gadolinium, this transition is visible between N=88 (near-spherical) and N=92 (well-deformed), with N=90 sitting at the critical point.

⭐

X(5) Critical Point Symmetry

X(5) is an analytical solution describing the critical point of the spherical-to-deformed shape phase transition. It predicts specific ratios of energy levels and electromagnetic transitions.

A nucleus is an X(5) candidate if its R₄/₂ ≈ 2.91 and its B(E2) transition ratio ≈ 1.58. ¹⁵⁴Gd satisfies both conditions remarkably well.

📐

Deviation Analysis (Δ and Q)

To quantify how well a symmetry describes a nucleus, two indicators are used: Δ (mean absolute deviation in keV) and Q (quality factor). A nucleus is classified under a symmetry when Δ ≤ 100 keV and Q ≤ 150.

These thresholds ensure the theoretical fit is physically meaningful and not merely numerical.

U(5) — Spherical Vibrator

Vibrational Limit

Nuclei near closed shells with nearly equal numbers of protons and neutrons. The nucleus vibrates around a spherical equilibrium shape. Energy levels form multiplets separated by a constant phonon energy ε.

R₄/₂ = 2.00 · β ≈ 0 · P < 4

O(6) — γ-Soft Nucleus

γ-Unstable Limit

The nucleus is deformed but unstable against γ-deformation — it can oscillate between oblate and prolate shapes. The energy spectrum is insensitive to the γ degree of freedom.

R₄/₂ = 2.50 · intermediate β · intermediate P

SU(3) — Axial Rotor

Rotational Limit

Well-deformed nuclei with axial symmetry. Energy levels follow a rotational pattern E ∝ L(L+1). The nucleus has a fixed, large prolate deformation and exhibits clear rotational bands.

R₄/₂ = 3.33 · β ≈ 0.3–0.4 · P > 6
Section 03
Research Results

¹⁵⁴Gd — X(5) Critical Point Candidate

Among all five isotopes studied, ¹⁵⁴Gd (N=90) stands out as the most physically interesting case. Its experimental energy ratio R₄/₂ = 3.01 closely matches the X(5) theoretical value of 2.91, and its B(E2) transition ratio Rₑ = 1.56 ± 0.06 lies near the expected X(5) value of ≈1.58. The P-factor of 4.632 is in the transitional range expected for a critical-point nucleus. These indicators collectively identify ¹⁵⁴Gd as a strong candidate for X(5) critical-point symmetry, residing at the boundary between spherical U(5) and deformed SU(3) phases.
